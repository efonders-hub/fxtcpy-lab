The Bleed Index

A structural scoring standard for VSL openings that measures how fast a video loses viewers before the offer lands, and tells you the exact reason why.

Every VSL opening does one of two things. It holds the viewer, or it leaks them.

Most leak.

The loss never shows on a dashboard in a way that names itself. Early exits register as low watch time, and low watch time gets treated as a traffic problem or a targeting problem or a creative problem.

The Bleed Index was built to close that gap.

It measures the structural conditions inside the opening itself, scores the leak rate, and names the exact mechanism causing the loss.

What the score is

The Bleed Index runs from 0 to 100.

Think of it as a leak rate. The higher the number, the more viewers the opening is losing before the offer reaches them.

Zero means the opening holds. One hundred means it hemorrhages.

0 – 19HealthyThe opening holds.
20 – 39LeakingMinor loss, worth a look.
40 – 64BleedingReal revenue is exiting the top of the funnel.
65 – 100HemorrhagingThe opening is losing most of its viewers before the pitch begins.

A score of 63, the rating assigned to the Gundry ProPlant VSL, shows that even a professionally produced, high-revenue opening can carry a significant structural leak. Read that teardown.

What it measures: the seven leaks

Every opening that bleeds, bleeds through one or more of seven structural leaks.

There are seven. Not five, not ten.

Seven, in a fixed order, because they map to the order a cold viewer forms belief.

1. Hook-Buyer Misalignment. The opening targets the wrong moment in the viewer's belief state. They arrive skeptical; the hook assumes curiosity.
2. Relevance Gap. The problem described floats above the viewer's felt experience. They recognize the category but do not feel named.
3. Authority Before Empathy. Credibility fires before the viewer feels understood. They get a resume when they needed recognition.
4. Proof Without Resolution. Social proof arrives before the viewer has resolved whether this is their problem. The testimony lands as a claim, not confirmation.
5. Logic Bridge Collapse. The transition from problem to solution breaks. The viewer is asked to take a leap between what hurts and what fixes it.
6. Momentum Drag. The script stalls. Side notes, tangents, and filler give the viewer a window to drift.
7. Identity Displacement. The opening talks to everyone and reaches no one. The right buyer does not recognize themselves.

How the score is built

Each of the seven leaks is graded for severity on the isolated opening, the first 500 to 1,000 words where the viewer decides whether to stay.

The primary leak is the one that reaches exit-grade earliest, the first place a cold viewer has a real reason to leave.

That earliest severe leak is the one costing you the most, so the score weighs it most heavily, then the next two behind it.

The same method runs against every VSL the same way, which is what makes one score comparable to another.

Where the number comes from

The Bleed Index is built from the FixThatCopy diagnostic archive: a growing library of real VSL openings, each graded against the same structural criteria, using the same named leaks, in the same sequence, every time.

That consistency is the point.

When every teardown follows the same framework, a score of 41 on one VSL means the same thing as a score of 41 on another, regardless of niche, price point, or format.

That comparability is what makes the Bleed Index a standard.
